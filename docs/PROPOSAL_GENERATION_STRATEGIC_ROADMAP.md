# Deal IQ AI — Proposal Generation Strategic Roadmap
### A senior post-deal M&A partner's review and a strategy to win the mandate

*Audience: product + engineering leadership. Lens: a Big4 / MBB partner who runs
post-deal engagements — PMI, separation, carve-out, JV stand-up, divestiture,
synergy and TSA — and who decides whether a proposal is good enough to put in
front of a client and win the work.*

---

## 1. The bar we are actually being judged against

A proposal does not win because it is well formatted. It wins because, inside
ninety seconds, the client's deal sponsor believes three things:

1. **"They understand *my* deal."** Not M&A in general — this target, this
   sector, this regulator, this deal structure, at this stage of the lifecycle.
2. **"Their numbers will survive my CFO and my IC."** Every figure is sourced,
   triangulated, and defensible under challenge.
3. **"This team will de-risk my outcome and I want them in the room."** There is
   a point of view, a recommendation they will stand behind, and proof they have
   done this before.

Today the engine reliably clears formatting and structure (post the deck
rework). It does **not yet reliably clear those three tests**. That is the gap
between a 7/10 "good template" and the 10/10 "this wins the mandate."

The rest of this document is the strategy to close it — deliberately framed as
**durable capabilities across deal types, sectors and geographies**, not as a
list of module tweaks.

---

## 2. Partner's critique of the current output

Reviewing the generated decks as I would a junior's first draft before it goes
to a client:

| What I see today | Why it costs the mandate |
|---|---|
| **Sector-generic reasoning.** Synergy %, multiples and levers come from broad benchmark tables. The Swiss Life / Telis deck reasons about "German financial advisory" but not about *tied-agent economics, BaFin conduct rules, Solvency II capital, IFA channel conflict*. | The client's own team knows their sector cold. Generic insight signals "they'll learn on my time." Instant credibility loss. |
| **Numbers without provenance.** "₹3.18B net run-rate synergy, 15.2% of EV, 13-month payback" appears with no build, no comparable, no source. | A CFO's first question is "says who, versus what?" Unsourced precision reads as *false* precision and gets the whole document discounted. |
| **One-size storyline regardless of deal type.** An acquisition, a carve-out, a JV and a separation are fundamentally different engagements, yet the section spine is largely shared. | A carve-out client cares about TSA exit and stranded cost; a JV client cares about governance deadlock and contribution parity. Showing the wrong concerns says "you don't do *this* kind of deal." |
| **Risk and conditions are templated, not deal-specific.** Kill-switches read plausibly but are not tied to *this* target's diligence reds. | Partners win on risk judgement. Generic risk = no judgement. |
| **No explicit "why us / why now."** The deck argues the deal; it does not argue *the engagement*. | Proposals are sold, not graded. Without a win theme and proof, even a correct analysis loses to a competitor who connected. |
| **Regulatory treated as a paragraph, not a gating path.** | In cross-border and regulated-sector deals (FS, healthcare, defence, telecom, energy) the regulatory path *is* the deal. Under-weighting it is disqualifying. |

None of these are formatting problems. They are **reasoning, evidence and
positioning** problems — which is exactly where partners add value and exactly
where a 10/10 is decided.

---

## 3. The strategic thesis

> **Stop generating "a proposal." Start generating *this partner's point of view
> on this specific deal* — conditioned on the deal's DNA, defended with evidence,
> and positioned to win the engagement.**

Four capability shifts make that real. They are sequenced as horizons because
each unlocks the next, but they are *capabilities*, not features — they cut
across every module, deal type and sector.

```
Deal DNA Conditioning  →  Evidence & Defensibility  →  Partner-Grade
(say the right things)    (make them believable)       Argumentation & Positioning
                                                        (make them win)
                                   ↑
                        Quality System & Knowledge Compounding
                        (make it repeatable and improving)
```

---

## 4. Horizon 1 — Deal-DNA Conditioning *(the highest-leverage move)*

**Principle:** every assertion the engine makes should be a function of the
deal's DNA, not a default. The platform already captures most of the DNA
(`deal-classifier`, `context-engine` deal_type/industry/geography, regulatory
screen) — today that signal *colours benchmarks* but does not yet *reshape the
argument*. It must drive the **storyline, the questions, the risks, the metrics
and the language**.

Define the deal's DNA along five axes and condition the entire document on them:

1. **Deal type / lifecycle stage** — acquisition, merger-of-equals, carve-out,
   separation, divestiture, JV, PE buyout; pre-signing vs. pre-close vs.
   post-close Day-1 vs. 100-day vs. steady-state.
2. **Sector** — and its *specific* value drivers, not a generic bucket.
3. **Geography / cross-border** — jurisdictions, FX, labour transfer regimes
   (TUPE/ARD), data residency, repatriation.
4. **Regulatory intensity** — merger control (CCI/CMA/EC/DOJ/CFIUS), sector
   regulators (BaFin/RBI/FCA/FDA/FERC), and what each *gates*.
5. **Strategic intent** — scale, scope, capability, geographic, defensive,
   roll-up — which determines where the value story must concentrate.

### What changes, by deal type (the spine must differ)

| Deal type | The 3 questions the client is actually asking | Sections that must lead |
|---|---|---|
| **Acquisition / PMI** | Will the synergies land and survive integration risk? | Synergy bridge, 100-day, integration risk, retention |
| **Carve-out** | What is stranded cost, and can we *exit TSAs* on time? | Perimeter & entanglements, TSA exit roadmap, stranded-cost takeout, standalone cost model |
| **Separation / Divestiture** | Can we separate cleanly without destroying value on either side? | Separation blueprint, Day-1 readiness, dis-synergy quantification, RemainCo operating model |
| **JV / Alliance** | Who controls what, and how do we avoid deadlock? | Governance & reserved matters, contribution & valuation parity, exit/deadlock mechanics, operating mandate |
| **Merger of equals** | Whose operating model wins, and how do we keep both talent pools? | Integration thesis, leadership/selection, culture & retention, governance |

A carve-out proposal that opens with an acquisition synergy bridge is *wrong* no
matter how pretty. Horizon 1 makes the spine itself deal-aware.

### Sector depth as a first-class asset
Replace broad benchmark rows with **sector knowledge packs**: for each priority
sector (FS/insurance, banking, healthcare/pharma, industrials, tech/software,
energy/utilities, consumer/retail, TMT), encode the real value levers, the
regulator(s) and what they gate, the standard diligence reds, the typical
synergy ranges *with their basis*, and the language insiders use. The
`context-engine` benchmark map is the seed; the strategic move is to deepen it
from "percent ranges" to "how value is actually created and lost in this
sector."

### Regulatory as a gating path, not a paragraph
Promote the regulatory screen into a **regulatory pathway**: which approvals,
in which order, on what timeline, with which remedy risk, and — critically —
*what each one gates in the plan* (Day-1 scope, clean-team boundaries, synergy
phasing). In FS, healthcare, defence, telecom and energy, and in any
cross-border deal, this is frequently the single most mandate-winning section.

**Why this is Horizon 1:** it is the difference between "understands M&A" and
"understands *my* deal." It reuses signal the platform already has, and it makes
every downstream section better.

## 5. Horizon 2 — Evidence & Defensibility

**Principle:** a partner never puts a number on a page they cannot defend.
Today the engine asserts; it must learn to **build, source and triangulate**.

1. **Every quantum gets a build.** Synergy, stranded cost, dis-synergy, TSA
   cost, retention exposure — each shown as a bottom-up build (driver × rate ×
   base), not a headline. The `deal-model` and synergy engines already compute
   structured numbers; surface the *bridge*, not just the total.
2. **Every number gets a basis line.** "₹2.52B cost synergy = 25% of ₹1.20B
   overlapping SG&A + procurement + IT, vs. sector precedent of 18–24%." A basis
   line converts false precision into defensible precision.
3. **Triangulation by comparable.** Tie each headline to the `comparables`
   engine: "in-line with [3 named precedents] at 12–16% synergy/EV." Partners
   win arguments with precedent.
4. **Confidence and sensitivity, honestly.** Show the number *and* its
   downside/upside and the 2–3 assumptions it is most sensitive to. IC members
   trust ranges with stated drivers far more than a single brittle point.
5. **Provenance for external claims.** Where live research is used, every market
   claim carries a citation; where it is modelled, it is labelled as modelled.
   Never blur the two.

**Outcome:** the deck survives the CFO and the IC. This is the test the current
output most visibly fails, and it is pure trust.

## 6. Horizon 3 — Partner-Grade Argumentation & Positioning

**Principle:** the same facts can lose or win depending on how they are argued
and whether they are positioned to sell the engagement.

1. **Pyramid, ruthlessly.** Answer first (the verdict), then the 3 MECE
   supports, then the proof. The storyline templates already encode this — wire
   the *content* to obey it, so the exec summary states the so-what, not a recap.
2. **One assertion per page, and make it the title.** (Now enforced in the
   deck.) Extend the discipline to the *body*: every chart and table earns its
   place by supporting the page's single claim.
3. **A real recommendation with conviction.** Conditional Go / Go / Pass — with
   the conditions and kill-switches tied to *this* deal's diligence reds and
   regulatory path, not a template. Partners are hired for judgement; the
   document must show it.
4. **The "why us / why now" win theme.** Add an explicit, deal-specific
   engagement case: the 2–3 reasons this risk profile needs *this* team now,
   backed by relevant proof points (sector, deal-type, geography credentials).
   A proposal is a sales document; today the engine forgets that.
5. **Counterparty and competitive tension awareness.** Acknowledge the other
   side's leverage, the auction dynamics, the alternatives — it signals
   real-world deal experience, which clients buy.
6. **Language calibrated to the reader.** Board vs. IC vs. CFO vs. integration
   lead read differently. Storyline + audience should set tone, density and
   vocabulary.

**Outcome:** the document moves from "correct analysis" to "this is the team and
the point of view I want." This is where the mandate is actually won or lost
between two competent firms.

## 7. Horizon 4 — Quality System & Knowledge Compounding *(makes it durable)*

A 10/10 cannot depend on a lucky generation. Build the machinery that makes
quality repeatable and *improving*:

1. **A partner-grade rubric as a gate, not a vibe.** Extend the existing
   `mbb-rubric` / `critique` layer into an explicit scorecard — *Deal-Specificity,
   Evidence, Argumentation, Risk Judgement, Win-Theme, Polish* — scored on every
   draft, with a minimum bar to ship and the weakest dimension named.
2. **Adversarial self-review (red-team pass).** Before output, a second pass
   plays "skeptical CFO / IC chair": flags every unsourced number, every generic
   claim, every templated risk, and forces a fix. This is how the banned-phrase
   gate should grow up — from string matching to *reasoning* critique.
3. **A reference library of won decks.** Curate exemplar outputs per deal
   type × sector; use them as few-shot anchors and as the visual/argument target.
   Quality compounds when the system learns from its best work.
4. **Closed feedback loop.** Capture partner edits and win/loss outcomes; feed
   the deltas back into prompts, rubric weights and the knowledge packs. The
   asset that compounds fastest wins the category.
5. **Model strategy that stays current.** (Now in place via active-model
   refresh.) Always route the *reasoning-heavy* proposal synthesis to the
   strongest available model per provider, and keep that selection self-healing
   as models evolve — quality of thought is upstream of everything above.

## 8. Sequencing and why this order

| Horizon | Capability | Why it's sequenced here | Primary leverage |
|---|---|---|---|
| **H1** | Deal-DNA conditioning | Reuses signal we already capture; makes everything downstream relevant | "Understands *my* deal" |
| **H2** | Evidence & defensibility | Relevance is worthless if the numbers don't survive challenge | "Survives my CFO/IC" |
| **H3** | Argumentation & positioning | Only worth doing once the content is right and defensible | "I want this team" |
| **H4** | Quality system & compounding | Locks the gains in and makes them improve over time | Durable 10/10 |

H1 is first because it is the highest-leverage, lowest-cost move — it converts
existing structured signal (deal type, sector, geography, regulatory) from
*decoration* into *the driver of the argument*. H4 runs in parallel from day one
as the measurement spine; you cannot improve what you do not score.

---

## 9. How we will know it worked

- **Partner test:** a post-deal partner scores a blind sample ≥ 8.5/10 on the
  rubric across acquisition, carve-out, separation and JV deals — *without*
  knowing which were AI-generated.
- **Defensibility test:** every headline number on every page has a build, a
  basis line and (where external) a citation.
- **Deal-fit test:** swapping the deal type visibly changes the spine, the
  questions, the risks and the language — not just the names.
- **Win test (the only one that ultimately matters):** proposals convert to
  mandates at a rate a partner would stake their name on.

---

## 10. One-line summary

> Today the engine produces a *well-structured generic proposal*. The strategy is
> to make it produce *a specific partner's defensible, well-argued, mandate-winning
> point of view on this exact deal* — by conditioning on deal DNA, grounding every
> number in evidence, arguing it like a partner, and scoring it relentlessly until
> it earns the 10/10 and the signature.
